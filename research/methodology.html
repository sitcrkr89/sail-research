Methodology

How a claim earns the right to be called fact.

Every Sail Research report runs on the same four-step discipline: capture the primary source with a verifiable hash, label each claim by how far it was verified, keep fact separate from model reasoning, and never publish without a human reviewing the trail. This page documents that discipline directly — not a marketing description of it.

1

Hashed Capture

When a primary source is pulled — a company newsroom page, an investor release, an official filing — we record the retrieval date, the byte size, and a SHA-256 hash of the captured content alongside the source URL. The hash makes the capture checkable: if the source page changes later, the hash proves what it said the day we read it.

Example, from the HBM tracking report (SR-2026-0005): each company's HBM4/HBM4E disclosure was captured 8 Jul 2026 and stored with retrieval date + SHA-256 hash before any claim was written into the report.
2

Evidence Labels

Inside a report, every claim carries one of three internal labels — Direct Evidence (verified against the primary source itself), Analytic Prior (a transparent, rule-based scoring or inference built on top of captured evidence, not a fact on its own), or Requires Verification (flagged as unconfirmed in the source data itself, stated as a gap rather than guessed at). These roll up into the public Evidence Grade shown on every report card:

A
Primary-Verified
Full-text sources, primary data, or first-party datasets verified by the operator.
B
Headline-Corroborated
Claims verified to headline level across multiple outlets. Article bodies not independently confirmed.
C
Hypothesis
Interpretive signals and projections. Explicitly labeled; never stated as fact.
3

The Fact / Hypothesis Boundary

A model's scoring, ranking, or scenario projection is never presented as a confirmed fact, and a confirmed fact is never dressed up with a confidence number it didn't earn. Every report that mixes the two — captured evidence plus a scoring layer — carries an explicit "Fact vs. Model Prior" box so a reader can see exactly where verification ends and interpretation begins.

What this boundary rules out: treating a zero score or a data gap as evidence of absence, stating a model's ranking as if it were a disclosed fact, or letting a hedge ("likely," "expected to") stand in for an actual source.
4

Operator Review

No report publishes on a pipeline's say-so. A human operator reviews the evidence trail — sources, hashes, labels, and the fact/hypothesis boundary — before a report goes live. If the trail doesn't support the grade, the grade comes down; we would rather publish a B than dress it up as an A. Published reports are then tracked for seven days, and what actually happened feeds the next topic selection.
